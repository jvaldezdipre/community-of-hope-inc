When building website mockups or landing pages, follow these principles every single time. No shortcuts, no generic AI output.

---

DESIGN PHILOSOPHY:

- Editorial, intentional design. Every element earns its place on the page.
- Massive spacing between sections (140-180px padding). Let content breathe. White space is not wasted space — it's the design.
- No emojis as icons. Ever. Use typography, numbering, or simple geometric elements instead.

- No floating cards, badge gimmicks, or decorative clutter. Clean means clean.
- Every section should feel like its own moment. A user lands on it, reads it, absorbs it, then moves on.
- The design must FEEL like the industry it represents. A construction company should feel solid, grounded, and industrial. A therapy practice should feel calm, safe, and warm. A restaurant should feel sensory and inviting. A law firm should feel authoritative and structured. The visual language — colors, weight, texture, spacing density, typography choices — should all reinforce what the business actually does. Someone should be able to tell what industry the site belongs to before reading a single word.
- A beautiful website that doesn't convert is a brochure. Every site must be beautiful AND strategic. Design serves the funnel, not the other way around.

---

CONVERSION ARCHITECTURE:
Before designing anything, answer these questions about the business:

1. WHO is the visitor? What are they feeling when they land on this page? What problem brought them here?
2. WHAT is the primary traffic source? Google search (solution-aware), social media ads (problem-aware), referrals (trust-primed), or a mix?
3. WHAT objections will stop them from taking action? Cost, trust, time, uncertainty, not knowing who they'll work with?
4. WHAT is the single most important action we want them to take? Call, fill out a form, book online, request a quote?

These answers determine the entire page structure — not aesthetics, not personal preference.

SOLUTION-AWARE vs PROBLEM-AWARE:
Every landing page must be built for the visitor's awareness level. This is the most important strategic decision before writing a single line of code.

SOLUTION-AWARE visitors (primary for most local service businesses):

- They already know what they want. They searched "therapist near me" or "roofing company Columbia SC" or "best Italian restaurant downtown."
- They're comparing you against 3-4 other tabs open in their browser.
- The page must: confirm they're in the right place FAST, prove trust immediately, remove objections quickly, and make the primary action easy to take.
- Above the fold: clear headline matching their search intent, social proof, key trust signals (insurance, certifications, ratings), and a visible CTA. No long warmup needed.
- The page structure should be: confirm → prove → differentiate → convert.
- Don't over-educate. They already know they need the service. Show them why YOU'RE the right choice.

PROBLEM-AWARE visitors (primary for paid social, content marketing, cold traffic):

- They know something is wrong but may not know the exact solution. "My back hurts all the time" not "chiropractor near me." "My website isn't getting leads" not "web designer."
- They need more warmup before they'll take action.
- The page must: speak to their pain first, connect pain to solution, build trust through education, then present the offer.
- Above the fold: headline addressing the pain point or desired outcome, empathetic sub-headline, softer CTA like "learn more" or "see how we help."
- The page structure should be: empathize → educate → prove → convert.
- More content, more storytelling, more gradual progression toward the CTA.

IMPORTANT: One business may need BOTH types of pages. The main website/homepage should match the primary traffic source (usually Google for local businesses = solution-aware). Separate landing pages can be built for ad campaigns targeting problem-aware traffic. Never try to serve both awareness levels on the same page — it dilutes both.

ABOVE THE FOLD (the most important real estate on the page):
Most visitors won't scroll far. The top of the page must do heavy lifting. For solution-aware pages, above the fold should include:

1. A headline that confirms the visitor is in the right place (match their search intent, not just emotional copy)
2. A sub-headline with the core value prop — what makes this business different from the other tabs
3. Social proof — star rating, review count, a short testimonial, or "trusted by X clients"
4. Key trust signals that answer make-or-break questions for the industry:
    - Therapy/Medical/Dental: insurance names (this determines if they can afford to say yes)
    - Construction/Trades: licensed, bonded, insured + free estimates
    - Legal: years of experience, case results, free consultation
    - Restaurant: hours, location, reservation availability
    - Auto: certifications (ASE), accepted payments
    - Identify what the visitor MUST confirm before going deeper, and put it above the fold
5. A clear primary CTA and a secondary path for people not ready yet
6. Phone number visible and prominent (not hidden in the nav)

For problem-aware pages, above the fold should include:

1. A headline addressing the pain point or desired outcome
2. Empathetic sub-headline
3. A softer CTA ("See how we help" / "Learn more") alongside the primary action
4. Social proof can sit slightly lower since trust is built through the content journey

FUNNEL LOGIC — EVERY SECTION HAS A JOB:
No section exists just to fill space or look nice. Each section must move the visitor closer to the primary action:

- Hero: Confirm they're in the right place (solution-aware) or speak to their pain (problem-aware). Dual-path CTAs: one for ready visitors, one for browsers.
- Trust bar / social proof: Immediately validate the business. Numbers, years, certifications, coverage area.
- Why Us / Differentiator: Answer "why should I pick you over the other options?" This must come EARLY on solution-aware pages. Most businesses skip this and it costs them.
- Testimonials: Real social proof with source branding (Google logo, Yelp logo, etc.) to prove reviews aren't fabricated. Include the service type under each testimonial so visitors self-identify. Place testimonials before services on solution-aware pages (trust before details).
- About: Build human connection. Keep it tight — 2-3 paragraphs max. End with a soft nudge CTA, not just information.
- Services: Help visitors self-qualify. Each service should include a "Good for:" or "This is for you if..." line so people see themselves in the offering. Include inline CTAs that appear on hover or at the end of each service.
- Team: Create personal connection. Each team member should have a "Book with [name]" link so the decision becomes personal. Include a "help me find my match" option for visitors who are overwhelmed by choice.
- FAQ: Remove objections. Order questions by what solution-aware visitors care about most (availability, process, cost) not what the business thinks is important. End with a nudge for unanswered questions — "Still have questions?" with call + message options.
- Mid-page CTA: Place a conversion bar after the testimonials section (or after the 3rd-4th section). This catches people who've seen enough and are ready to act without scrolling to the bottom.
- Intake form / Primary conversion: Keep on the page (embedded), not an external portal link. Every click away from the site is a lost lead. Include benefit bullets next to the form (response time, what happens next, no commitment). The form should feel easy, not like paperwork.
- Final CTA: Offer an alternative conversion path for people who don't like forms. "Prefer to talk? Call us." Catches the last group.
- Sticky mobile CTA: Always present on mobile. Two buttons: Call + Primary Action. Fixed to the bottom of the screen.

SELF-QUALIFYING COPY:
Service descriptions should help visitors identify which service is right for them without needing to call and ask. Use patterns like:

- "Good for: anxiety, depression, trauma, life transitions"
- "Best if you need: roof replacement, storm damage repair, new construction"
- "This is for you if: your family is navigating conflict or major changes"

This reduces friction because the visitor thinks "that's me" before they ever fill out a form.

TESTIMONIAL STRATEGY:

- Always include a testimonial/reviews section. Zero social proof is a conversion killer.
- Use real reviews when available — pull from Google, Yelp, Alignable, or industry-specific platforms.
- Include the platform logo (Google "G", Yelp icon, etc.) next to the star rating in each testimonial card. This signals legitimacy — visitors know these aren't fabricated.
- Under each testimonial, include the service type ("Individual Therapy", "Roof Replacement", "Family Dinner") so visitors self-identify with the reviewer.
- If the business has no reviews yet, include placeholder testimonial cards that show the client what the section WILL look like, and note it's a placeholder. This itself is a selling point for the redesign — "your new site is ready for reviews the moment you get them."
- Three testimonials is the sweet spot. More than four starts feeling like padding.

CTA LANGUAGE:

- Primary CTA should be specific and low-friction. "Request a Free Consultation" converts better than "Schedule a Session" because it implies no commitment.
- Secondary CTA should catch browsers: "See how we help" / "Not sure yet? Learn more" / "Take the quiz"
- Match CTA language to the industry:
    - Therapy: "Request a Free Consultation" / "Reach Out" / "Start Your Journey"
    - Construction: "Get a Free Estimate" / "Request a Quote" / "See Our Work"
    - Restaurant: "Make a Reservation" / "See the Menu" / "Order Now"
    - Legal: "Schedule a Free Case Review" / "Talk to an Attorney"
    - Fitness: "Start Your Free Trial" / "Claim Your Spot" / "Get Started Today"

FORM STRATEGY:

- Embed the primary intake/contact form on the page. Never rely solely on an external booking portal.
- Keep forms short: name, email, phone, one dropdown for service type, one optional text field. Every additional field reduces completion rate.
- Include a "Not sure" option in service dropdowns: "Not sure yet — help me decide." This captures leads who would otherwise bounce.
- Include a "Preferred [team member]" dropdown when applicable (therapists, attorneys, stylists). This makes the decision personal and reduces follow-up friction.
- Below the submit button: "Your information is secure and confidential" or similar trust language.
- After the form, show small benefit badges: "Response within 24hrs" / "We verify insurance" / "No commitment required"

INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC DESIGN DIRECTION:
Before writing any code, define the visual personality based on the client's industry:

- Healthcare / Therapy / Wellness: Soft, calming palette. Airy spacing. Gentle serif headings. Light font weights. Rounded but subtle UI elements. Feels like a deep breath. Key trust signals above the fold: insurance names, licensure, statewide/local coverage.
- Construction / Trades / Industrial: Bold, heavy typography. Dark backgrounds with high contrast. Tight grids. Raw textures like concrete or steel tones. Feels like something built to last. Key trust signals: licensed/bonded/insured, years in business, project count.
- Restaurants / Food / Hospitality: Rich, warm tones. Sensory imagery focus. Elegant or playful type depending on the restaurant tier. Moody lighting feel. The site should make you hungry or thirsty. Key trust signals: review ratings, hours, location.
- Law / Finance / Professional Services: Structured, authoritative. Strong vertical rhythm. Conservative palette with navy, charcoal, deep greens. Serif-heavy. Feels like a firm handshake. Key trust signals: case results, years of experience, bar admissions.
- Creative / Design / Photography: Maximalist or dramatically minimal. Large imagery. Experimental layouts. Type as art. The site IS the portfolio.
- Tech / SaaS / Startups: Clean geometric type. Systematic spacing. Accent gradients or neon pops. Data-forward layouts. Feels precise and modern.
- Real Estate: Luxury-leaning. Large property imagery. Gold or earth tone accents. Map integrations. Aspirational headline copy. Feels like an upgrade.
- Fitness / Sports: High energy. Bold sans-serifs. Dark mode default. Dynamic angles or diagonal elements. Action-oriented CTAs. Feels like momentum.
- Beauty / Salon / Spa: Refined elegance. Thin serif or stylish sans type. Muted pinks, nudes, soft golds. Delicate borders and fine lines. Feels luxurious and personal.
- Automotive / Mechanic: Mechanical feel. Monospace or industrial type accents. Dark with red, orange, or yellow pops. Grid-heavy. Feels like a well-tuned machine.
- Education / Tutoring: Approachable and clear. Friendly sans-serifs. Bright but not childish accents. Organized information hierarchy. Feels trustworthy and smart.
- Church / Nonprofit: Warm and communal. Inviting photography feel. Earth tones or muted jewel tones. Generous spacing. Feels like belonging.
- Cleaning / Home Services: Fresh and bright. Light backgrounds, crisp blues or greens. Simple layouts. Before/after visual contrast. Feels spotless.
- Pet Services / Veterinary: Friendly and warm. Rounded type. Natural or playful colors. Approachable without being childish. Feels caring.

If the industry isn't listed, study real high-end websites in that space and define 3-4 adjectives that capture how the site should FEEL before designing anything. Then make every design decision serve those adjectives.

TYPOGRAPHY:

- Always pair a distinctive serif or display font for headings with a clean modern sans-serif for body text.
- Never reuse the same font combination twice across different client projects. Every client gets a unique pairing.
- Never use Inter, Roboto, Arial, Open Sans, or system fonts. Those are the hallmark of lazy design.
- Before selecting fonts, browse Google Fonts or similar and pick something that fits the CLIENT'S industry and vibe:
    - A law firm feels different than a bakery. A therapy practice feels different than a tech startup. The fonts should reflect that.
    - For calm/wellness: softer serifs with rounded terminals
    - For corporate/professional: structured serifs with geometric sans
    - For creative/bold: high-contrast display faces with minimal body fonts
    - For modern/tech: clean geometric or grotesk pairings
    - For trades/construction: heavy slab serifs or bold condensed sans
    - For food/hospitality: expressive display or script accents with readable body
    - For luxury/beauty: thin high-contrast serifs with elegant sans
- Pull from the full range available — there are hundreds of quality fonts. Never default to the same 5-10 popular choices.
- If you've used a font on a previous project in this conversation, pick a different one.
- Headings should be large and confident — clamp(2.4rem, 4vw, 3.4rem) minimum for section titles
- Body text should be light weight (300-400), generous line-height (1.8-2.0), and muted color — not black, something like #5A5A5A
- Use font-size, weight, letter-spacing, and case (uppercase labels at 0.68-0.72rem with wide tracking) to create clear visual hierarchy without relying on bold or color

---

COLOR:

- Pull the client's actual brand colors from their existing site/logo. Don't invent a palette.
- Use the brand color sparingly — as an accent, not a wash. Labels, hover states, key numbers, one or two interactive elements.
- Backgrounds should be subtle: off-white (#FAFAFA), true white for alternating sections, and one dark section (deep version of brand color) for contrast
- Borders and dividers should be barely visible (#EBEBEB or lighter)
- The overall color temperature should match the industry. Warm industries (food, wellness, hospitality) get warmer neutrals. Cool industries (tech, law, finance) get cooler neutrals.

---

LAYOUT:

- Max content width of 1100-1280px, centered
- Use CSS Grid with intentional asymmetry — sticky left columns with scrolling right content, split headers with description on the opposite side
- Thin 1px dividers between sections and rows to create rhythm
- No rounded corners over 10px. Keep it sharp and editorial. Exception: industries where softness is part of the brand (wellness, beauty, childcare) can go up to 16px.

---

INTERACTIONS:

- Scroll-triggered reveal animations: translateY(50px) to 0 with opacity, using cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1) easing, 1s duration
- Stagger delays on grouped elements (0.12s increments)
- Hover states should be subtle — color shifts, slight translateY, padding shifts. Not scale transforms or shadows appearing from nowhere.
- FAQ accordions with minimal +/- toggle, not chevrons
- For high-energy industries (fitness, automotive, nightlife), animations can be faster and more aggressive. For calm industries (therapy, spa, church), keep animations slower and gentler.
- Team card hover: subtle lift + shadow + image zoom (scale 1.04) for cards with real photos

---

CONTENT APPROACH:

- Use the client's actual business information — names, service descriptions, phone numbers, addresses — pulled from their real website
- Use the client's actual team photos. Never use initials placeholders when real headshots exist on their current site.
- Rewrite their copy to be warmer and more human while keeping the facts accurate
- Headlines should speak to the visitor's emotional state or desired outcome, not describe the business
- The tone of the copy should match the industry:
    - Therapy: empathetic, gentle, reassuring
    - Construction: confident, direct, no-nonsense
    - Restaurant: sensory, evocative, inviting
    - Law firm: authoritative, precise, reassuring
    - Fitness: motivating, action-oriented, bold
- Section labels should be tiny uppercase with wide letter-spacing, always above the heading
- Keep paragraph text to 2-3 sentences max per block. Break up walls of text.

STRUCTURE:
The following sections are typical for a service business site. Include all relevant sections but NEVER use the same layout pattern twice in a row on the same page, and NEVER use the same layout arrangements across different client projects.

REQUIRED SECTIONS (for a solution-aware service business page):

1. Hero (confirm search intent + social proof + trust signals + dual-path CTA)
2. Trust bar (numbers: years, team size, coverage, credentials count)
3. Why Us / Differentiator (what makes them different from competitors — this must come EARLY)
4. About the business (brief, with soft nudge CTA at the end)
5. Testimonials with source branding (Google logo, Yelp, etc.)
6. Mid-page CTA bar (catches people who've seen enough)
7. Services with self-qualifying copy ("Good for:" lines)
8. Team with individual booking links and matching prompt
9. One dark/contrasting accent section (credentials, methodology, or approach — kept concise)
10. FAQ ordered by conversion impact (availability first, then process, then policies)
11. Insurance/pricing/packages (if applicable — with key names also shown above the fold)
12. Embedded intake form with benefit bullets
13. Final CTA with alternative conversion path (phone for people who don't like forms)
14. Sticky mobile CTA bar (fixed bottom, call + primary action)
15. Footer

For problem-aware pages, reorder to: Hero (pain-focused) → Education/Problem → Solution introduction → Social proof → Services → Team → Form → CTA

LAYOUT VARIATION RULES:

- Alternate between centered, left-aligned, right-aligned, and asymmetric layouts across sections. If the hero is centered, the about section should NOT be centered.
- Mix these layout patterns throughout the page and across projects:
    - Split screen (text left / visual right, or flipped)
    - Full-width centered with tight max-width
    - Asymmetric grid (narrow left column / wide right, or 60/40, 70/30)
    - Staggered or offset columns where content doesn't line up on a perfect grid
    - Full-bleed sections breaking out of the max-width container
    - Overlapping elements where one section slightly bleeds into the next
    - Horizontal scrolling rows for tags, logos, or team members on certain projects
    - Bento grid layouts mixing different sized blocks
    - Numbered vertical list rows (full-width, one item per row)
    - Masonry or staggered card layouts instead of uniform grids
- For services specifically, rotate between these across projects:
    - Full-width numbered rows with self-qualifying "Good for:" copy
    - Two-column offset cards
    - Accordion/expandable list
    - Bento grid with mixed sizing
    - Tabbed or filtered view
    - Grid with inline CTAs per service
- For team sections, rotate between:
    - Standard card grid with booking links per team member
    - Horizontal scroll strip
    - Large featured layout (one big, rest small)
    - List layout with photo left and bio right
    - Staggered/offset grid where cards don't perfectly align
- For hero sections, rotate between:
    - Centered text with social proof and insurance/trust signals below CTAs
    - Split screen with image or visual on one side, form on the other
    - Left-aligned text with background visual element
    - Full-screen image/video background with overlay text
    - Asymmetric with text offset to one side and large typography
- Never stack more than two centered sections in a row. Break it up with a split layout, asymmetric grid, or left-aligned section.
- The overall page should have visual rhythm — tight sections followed by spacious ones, light backgrounds alternating with dark or tinted ones, full-width moments breaking up contained content.
- Every project should feel like a different designer built it while maintaining the same quality standard.

---

WHAT TO NEVER DO:

- No emoji icons anywhere in the design
- No generic stock photo placeholders with leaf/plant emojis
- No purple-gradient-on-white default AI aesthetic
- No card grids with colored icon circles for services
- No "floating cards" with shadows as decorative hero elements
- No more than 10px border-radius on anything (unless the industry calls for softness)
- No Inter, Roboto, or system font stacks
- No cookie-cutter component library look
- No excessive bold text or ALL CAPS body copy
- No walls of text without breathing room
- No making every website look the same regardless of industry
- No reusing the same layout structure, font pairing, or section arrangements from a previous project
- No initials placeholders when real team photos exist on the client's site
- No brochure-style sites that look good but don't guide visitors toward action
- No external booking portal links as the only conversion path — always embed a form on the page
- No burying the intake form at the very bottom without mid-page CTAs to catch ready visitors
- No service descriptions without self-qualifying copy that helps visitors see themselves in the offering
- No testimonials without source branding (Google, Yelp, etc.) — unbranded reviews feel fake
- No hero sections that prioritize emotional copy over confirming search intent (for solution-aware pages)
- No hiding insurance, certifications, or key trust signals deep in the page when they're make-or-break decision factors

---

Every site should look like it was designed by a human with taste who understands the client's industry AND their customer's decision-making process — not generated by AI. The constant across all projects is the quality of spacing, typography, restraint, and conversion thinking. Everything else — fonts, layouts, color temperature, animation speed, visual density, section ordering — should be tailored to who the business is, who they serve, and how those people make decisions.
